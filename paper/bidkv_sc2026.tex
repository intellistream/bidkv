% Abstract

\begin{abstract}
Online large language model (LLM) serving faces dynamic KV cache pressure
caused by heterogeneous request lengths, bursty arrivals, and growing context
windows.  When memory pressure exceeds capacity, the serving engine must
\emph{preempt} one or more running requests to reclaim KV
cache---yet existing policies select victims with little
awareness of cross-request heterogeneity.  vLLM defaults to
LIFO ordering; token-level heuristics such as H2O apply a
fixed scoring rule to every request, without coordinating
preemption decisions across the batch.

We present \bidkv, a bid-driven preemption scheduling primitive
that introduces \emph{explicit preemption-sensitivity signals}
(bids) as a first-class scheduling input.  Each active request
generates bids encoding the tokens freed upon
preemption~($\tokfree$) and a surrogate scheduling
cost~($\qualdelta$) derived from token-importance scores.
A constrained greedy solver ranks bids by the utility ratio
$\utilfn = \tokfree / (\qualdelta + \stabeps)$ and selects
victims that reclaim the most KV capacity per unit of
estimated scheduling disruption.  Under the recompute-fallback
execution model used in this work, preempted requests are
fully recomputed by the framework's native mechanism, so final
output quality is identical across all strategies; the
differentiation lies in scheduling efficiency---which requests
are preempted, how often, and at what recomputation cost.

\bidkv is framework-portable: a four-layer adapter abstraction
enables integration with vLLM and SGLang without modifying
their source code.  We evaluate \bidkv with
Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct on an NVIDIA RTX A6000 using ShareGPT
traces under Poisson arrival.  Across seven strategies forming
a complete attribution chain, \bidkv achieves
\bidkvSLOAttainment SLO attainment at medium load
(\bidkvSLOGainOverHtwoO above H2O-Style) and reduces P95~TTFT
by \bidkvTTFTReductionOverHtwoO at high load, while
maintaining equivalent throughput.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

Large language models (LLMs) based on the Transformer decoder
architecture~\cite{Vaswani2017}, including GPT-3~\cite{Brown2020},
PaLM~\cite{Chowdhery2023}, Llama~\cite{Touvron2023, Dubey2024}, and
Mistral~\cite{Jiang2023}, have become the backbone of interactive AI services.
Deploying these models as always-on inference services introduces a critical
systems challenge: \emph{managing the key-value (KV) cache that stores
attention state for every in-flight request}~\cite{Kwon2023, Pope2023}.
Because KV cache occupancy grows linearly with sequence length, the aggregate
memory demand of a concurrent batch fluctuates on a sub-second timescale as
requests arrive, generate tokens, and complete asynchronously.

When KV cache pressure exceeds available capacity, the serving engine must
\emph{preempt} one or more running requests---evicting their KV state to free
memory for other requests.  This raises a fundamental scheduling question:
\textbf{which request should be evicted, and at what cost?}

The answer has significant performance implications.  Preempting a
nearly completed request wastes the computation already invested and
triggers expensive recomputation; preempting a request that has just
begun frees substantial KV capacity at low recomputation cost.
Yet existing systems select victims with little or no consideration
of these trade-offs:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,nosep]
\item \textbf{Request-level preemption}~\cite{Kwon2023, Yu2022}: vLLM's
  PagedAttention preempts entire requests under memory pressure using LIFO
  ordering---the most recently arrived request is evicted first, regardless
  of its progress or the cost of recomputation.

\item \textbf{Token-level heuristics}~\cite{Zhang2023h2o, Xiao2024,
  Liu2023scissorhands, Li2024snapkv}: schemes such as H2O and StreamingLLM
  score tokens within a single request, but when repurposed to guide
  preemption decisions they apply a \emph{fixed scoring rule} identically to
  every request, without coordinating across the batch or accounting for
  heterogeneous recomputation costs.

\item \textbf{System-level scheduling}~\cite{Yu2022, Agrawal2024sarathiserve,
  Zhong2024}: Orca-style iteration-level scheduling and prefill--decode
  disaggregation optimize batching granularity but do not address KV cache
  preemption decisions directly.
\end{itemize}

\noindent
Across all three categories, we identify a structural gap: no existing system
provides an \emph{explicit signal pathway} through which upper layers---be they
applications, SLO policies, or scheduling heuristics---can communicate
per-request preemption sensitivity to the runtime; nor do they perform
cross-request coordination when selecting victims.

\paragraph{Key insight.}
We observe that effective preemption scheduling under heterogeneous load
benefits from \emph{explicit preemption-sensitivity signals}---what we term
\emph{bids}---that flow from a scoring layer into the runtime scheduler.
A bid encodes, for a given request, the tokens that would be freed upon
preemption~($\tokfree$) and a surrogate scheduling cost~($\qualdelta$) derived
from token-importance scores.  $\qualdelta$ does not directly measure
downstream output quality; rather, it captures heterogeneity in how costly
preempting a particular request would be from a scheduling perspective.
Given bids from all active requests, the scheduler solves a constrained
optimization: select victims that maximize freed tokens subject to a
cumulative cost budget~$\dbudget$.  A simple \emph{utility-ratio greedy
solver}---ranking bids by $\utilfn = \tokfree / (\qualdelta + \stabeps)$ and
selecting greedily---yields effective decisions in microseconds.

At a conceptual level, the bid abstraction opens an explicit signal pathway
from upper layers into the preemption scheduler, decoupling \emph{what} to
optimize (the scoring strategy) from \emph{how} to optimize it (the solver).
In the present work, we instantiate this pathway with surrogate bids derived
from H2O-style attention-weight scores~\cite{Zhang2023h2o}.  Under the
recompute-fallback execution model, preempted requests are fully recomputed by
the framework's native mechanism (\eg vLLM's
\texttt{\_preempt\_request()}), so \emph{final output quality is identical
across all strategies}.  The differentiation lies in scheduling
efficiency---which requests are preempted, how often, and at what
recomputation cost to the system.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{C\arabic*.},leftmargin=*,nosep]
\item \textbf{Bid-based preemption-sensitivity mechanism.}  We introduce an
  explicit signal (\emph{bid}) that allows scoring strategies or application
  policies to convey per-request preemption sensitivity to the scheduler,
  replacing the implicit, one-size-fits-all policies of existing systems.

\item \textbf{Scorer-agnostic scheduling architecture.}  We design a
  scoring-to-bid layer (any scorer satisfying a \texttt{score()} protocol) and a
  constrained greedy solver that decouples token-level importance scoring from
  request-level victim selection.

\item \textbf{Framework-portable adapter.}  We define a
  \texttt{Framework\-Adapter} ABC and demonstrate integration with
  vLLM~\cite{Kwon2023} and SGLang~\cite{Zheng2024sglang}
  without modifying their source code.

\item \textbf{Comprehensive attribution evaluation.}  We evaluate seven
  strategies that form a complete attribution chain isolating the contribution
  of each design choice---scheduling coordination, token scoring, and explicit
  bids---under controlled workloads and frozen traces.
\end{enumerate}

% §2  Background and Motivation
% ============================================================
\section{Background and Motivation}\label{sec:background}

\subsection{KV Cache in LLM Serving}\label{sec:bg:kvcache}

Autoregressive Transformer decoders~\cite{Vaswani2017} compute attention as
$\text{Attn}(Q,K,V) = \text{softmax}(QK^\top / \sqrt{d_k})\,V$, caching the
key and value projections of all previously generated tokens.  For a model with
$L$~layers, $H$~attention heads, and head dimension~$d_h$, the KV cache of a
single request of length~$n$ occupies $2LHd_h n$ elements.  With bf16
precision, Llama-3.1-8B ($L{=}32, H{=}8_{\text{kv}}, d_h{=}128$) allocates
128\,KiB per token per request.

Modern serving systems such as vLLM~\cite{Kwon2023} manage KV cache via
PagedAttention, allocating physical memory in fixed-size \emph{blocks} mapped
through a block table.  Despite this optimization, KV memory scales linearly
with sequence length: a single 4096-token request occupies $\sim$512\,MiB,
placing severe pressure on the $\sim$32\,GiB of KV-allocatable memory on a
48\,GiB A6000.

\subsection{The Preemption Problem}\label{sec:bg:preemption}

When KV cache utilization exceeds capacity, the serving engine must
\emph{preempt} running requests to free memory.  vLLM implements preemption via
\texttt{\_preempt\_request()}: the victim's KV blocks are released, and the
request re-enters the waiting queue for recomputation from scratch.  The default
victim selection is LIFO---the most recently scheduled request is evicted
first---which ignores request progress and recomputation cost.

Consider three concurrent requests under memory pressure:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,nosep]
\item $R_1$: 128-token chat reply, 90\% generation complete.
  Preempting wastes nearly all invested computation.
\item $R_2$: 2048-token document summary, 10\% complete.
  Preempting frees significant KV at low recomputation cost.
\item $R_3$: 512-token code completion, 50\% complete.
  Moderate recomputation cost.
\end{itemize}

\noindent
LIFO preempts whichever request was most recently scheduled, regardless of
these differences.  A scheduler aware of per-request recomputation cost would
preempt~$R_2$ first: it frees the most tokens at the lowest relative cost,
yielding the highest utility ratio.  This example illustrates two requirements
that motivate our design:

\begin{description}[style=unboxed,leftmargin=0em,nosep]
\item[R1 --- Explicit preemption-sensitivity signals.]
  The scheduler should accept per-request signals that capture heterogeneity in
  preemption cost, rather than relying solely on arrival order or a single
  fixed heuristic.

\item[R2 --- Cross-request coordination.]
  Victim selection should consider the \emph{batch-level} trade-off---how much
  KV capacity is freed versus how much scheduling disruption is
  incurred---rather than scoring each request in isolation.

\item[R3 --- Scorer-agnostic design.]
  Because the best importance signal may vary across workloads and models, the
  scoring strategy (\eg attention weights, uniform, random) should be
  pluggable without changing the selection algorithm.

\item[R4 --- Framework portability.]
  The mechanism should integrate with heterogeneous engines (\eg vLLM, SGLang)
  without requiring source-code modifications, to facilitate adoption across
  the serving ecosystem.
\end{description}

% ============================================================
% §3  Related Work
% ============================================================
\section{Related Work}\label{sec:related}

We organize related work into three categories and position \bidkv relative to
each.

\paragraph{A --- Request-level scheduling and preemption.}
vLLM~\cite{Kwon2023} introduced PagedAttention with request-level preemption
(swap or recompute) as the fallback under memory pressure.
Orca~\cite{Yu2022} pioneered iteration-level scheduling but does not manage KV
capacity.
DistServe~\cite{Zhong2024} and Splitwise~\cite{Patel2024} disaggregate prefill
and decode phases, while
Sarathi-Serve~\cite{Agrawal2024sarathiserve} introduces chunked prefills.
FlexGen~\cite{Sheng2023flexgen} offloads KV cache to CPU/disk.
LoongServe~\cite{Wu2024loongserve} scales to long contexts via elastic
partitioning.
Prompt Cache~\cite{Gim2024} and Hydragen~\cite{Juravsky2024} exploit prefix
sharing to reduce redundant computation.
These systems advance batching, placement, and memory management, but their
preemption policies remain order-based (LIFO or FCFS) and do not incorporate
per-request cost signals when selecting victims.

\paragraph{B --- Token-level KV cache compression.}
H2O~\cite{Zhang2023h2o} identifies ``heavy-hitter'' tokens via
cumulative attention scores.
Streaming\-LLM~\cite{Xiao2024} preserves attention-sink tokens
for infinite streaming.
Scissorhands~\cite{Liu2023scissorhands} exploits persistence-of-importance.
SnapKV~\cite{Li2024snapkv} clusters attention features to compress prefix
cache.
KIVI~\cite{Liu2024kivi} uses asymmetric 2-bit KV quantization.
PyramidKV~\cite{Zhang2024pyramidkv} applies pyramid budgets across layers.
These methods effectively reduce per-request KV footprint, but each applies a
fixed budget and scoring rule to every request independently.  They do not
coordinate across the batch to reason about which \emph{request} to target, nor
do they expose a hook for external policies to influence the decision.
\bidkv is complementary: its scoring layer can wrap any token-level scorer (\eg
H2O) to derive per-request bid signals for cross-request coordination.

\paragraph{C --- System-level QoS and scheduling theory.}
Dominant Resource Fairness~\cite{Ghodsi2011} and multi-resource
packing~\cite{Grandl2014} optimize heterogeneous cluster resources.
The knapsack literature~\cite{Martello1990, Kellerer2004} provides algorithmic
foundations for constrained selection.
These frameworks operate on compute or network resources, not KV cache capacity,
and do not incorporate the per-request preemption-sensitivity signals that arise
in LLM serving.

\paragraph{Positioning.}
To our knowledge, no prior work provides an explicit, scorer-agnostic signal
pathway through which per-request preemption sensitivity can be communicated to
the KV cache scheduler and used for cross-request victim coordination.
\bidkv aims to fill this gap: it is complementary to token-level compression
methods (which supply scoring signals) and to request-level scheduling systems
(which supply the preemption execution mechanism).

\section{Design}\label{sec:design}

\bidkv organizes its logic into four layers (Figure~\ref{fig:architecture}): the
\emph{Runtime Adapter Layer} detects KV pressure and executes preemptions via
the framework's native mechanism; the \emph{Scoring-to-Bid Layer} translates
token-level scores into structured bids; the \emph{Constrained Solver Layer}
selects preemption victims; and the \emph{Bid Signal Layer} defines the shared
data structures used across all layers.

\input{figures/fig1_architecture}

\subsection{Bid Signal Layer}\label{sec:design:bid}

The atomic unit of communication is the \emph{scheduling bid}, a frozen
(immutable) data class with the following fields:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,nosep]
\item \texttt{bid\_id} --- unique identifier.
\item \texttt{request\_id} --- the inference request this bid applies to.
\item $\tokfree$ (\texttt{tokens\_freed}) --- the number of KV tokens freed if
  this request is preempted.
\item $\qualdelta$ (\texttt{quality\_delta}) --- a surrogate scheduling cost
  $\in [0, 1]$ derived from token importance scores.  Lower values indicate
  requests whose preemption incurs less scheduling disruption.
\item \texttt{confidence} $\in [0, 1]$ --- the scorer's confidence in
  $\qualdelta$.
\item \texttt{metadata} --- strategy-specific information.
\end{itemize}

Each bid carries a derived \emph{utility} property:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:utility}
  \utilfn(b) \;=\; \frac{b.\tokfree}{b.\qualdelta + \stabeps},
  \qquad \stabeps = 10^{-3}.
\end{equation}
A high utility ratio means preempting this request frees many tokens for low
scheduling cost.

Bids are collected in a \texttt{BidPool}---an immutable snapshot of all active
bids at a given instant.  The solver output is a \texttt{BidAcceptance}
containing the accepted bid IDs, total freed tokens, and cumulative cost.

\subsection{Scoring-to-Bid Layer}\label{sec:design:scoring}

A core design principle is \emph{scorer-agnosticism}: the system does not
prescribe which token importance metric to use.  It defines a minimal
\texttt{ScoringStrategy} protocol:

\begin{quote}
{\small\texttt{score(token\_ids: Sequence[int], **ctx) -> list[float]}}
\end{quote}

\noindent
Any object satisfying this protocol---returning per-token importance scores in
$[0, 1]$---can serve as a scorer.  We implement four strategies:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,nosep]
\item \textbf{H2OScoring}~\cite{Zhang2023h2o}: cumulative attention-weight
  scoring.  The production-intended scorer.
\item \textbf{AttentionWeightScoring}: direct attention-weight extraction.
\item \textbf{UniformScoring}: all tokens receive score~$0.5$.  Ablation
  baseline.
\item \textbf{RandomScoring}: i.i.d.\ uniform random. Ablation baseline.
\end{itemize}

Given scores, \texttt{build\_bids()} generates bids for a request at multiple
scheduling aggressiveness levels $\ell \in \{0.2, 0.4, 0.6\}$:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
\item Sort tokens by importance in ascending order.
\item Let $\tokfree = \lfloor n \cdot \ell \rfloor$.
\item Compute $\qualdelta = \frac{1}{\tokfree} \sum_{j=1}^{\tokfree}
  s_{\pi(j)}$, where $\pi$ is the ascending permutation.
\item Emit a bid with these values.
\end{enumerate}

The mean importance of removed tokens serves as a surrogate scheduling cost.
Because low-importance tokens are counted first, $\qualdelta$ increases
sub-linearly with $\ell$, favoring moderate preemption.

\textbf{Interpretation under recompute fallback.}  In the execution model used
in this work, preempted requests are fully recomputed---output quality is
preserved regardless of which request is selected.  The surrogate $\qualdelta$
therefore does not represent actual quality degradation; it serves as a
\emph{ranking signal} that captures cross-request heterogeneity in token
importance distributions, enabling the solver to identify requests whose
preemption has the least system-level impact.

The solver enforces two constraints:
\begin{description}[style=unboxed,leftmargin=0em,nosep]
\item[At-most-one bid per request:]  Prevents double-counting freed tokens.
\item[Scheduling cost budget:]  The cumulative $\qualdelta$ must not exceed
  $\dbudget$ (default~$0.15$), controlling preemption aggressiveness.
\end{description}

\noindent
The algorithm runs in $O(\numbids \log \numbids)$ time.  In practice
$\numbids < 100$, so solving takes microseconds---negligible compared with a
decode step.

\paragraph{Relation to knapsack problems.}
The victim selection problem is a variant of the 0-1 knapsack with a conflict
graph and a budget constraint.  While the general problem is NP-hard, the greedy
approach achieves strong empirical performance because the utility ratio provides
an effective ranking signal and the number of items is
small~\cite{Martello1990, Kellerer2004}.

\subsection{Runtime Adapter Layer}\label{sec:design:adapter}

To achieve framework portability~(R4), \bidkv defines a
\texttt{Framework\-Adapter} ABC with five responsibilities:

\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
\item \textbf{KV stats visibility}: returns current and maximum token counts
  for pressure detection.
\item \textbf{Pressure-event interception}: intercepts the framework's
  native preemption path and invokes \bidkv's solver first.
\item \textbf{Preemption execution}: delegates to the framework's
  native mechanism (\eg vLLM \texttt{\_preempt\_request()}).
\item \textbf{Scoring callback}: feeds attention weights or other signals back
  to the scoring strategy after each decode step.
\item \textbf{Lifecycle management}: cleans up bids and state when a request
  completes.
\end{enumerate}

This contract is intentionally minimal: any serving framework exposing KV
statistics and a preemption hook can implement the adapter.

\section{Implementation}\label{sec:impl}

\bidkv is implemented as a standalone Python package with \textbf{zero external
dependencies} (Python $\geq$~3.10 standard library only), totaling
$\sim$12,000~lines of code and \numTests unit tests.  The system defaults to
\textbf{off} (\texttt{BidKVConfig(enabled=False)}) and includes a global
\emph{kill switch} (\texttt{kill\_switch=True}) for instant bypass.  Seven
strategies are registered through a \texttt{BaselineRegistry}; adding a new
strategy requires one \texttt{register()} call.

\subsection{vLLM Integration}\label{sec:impl:vllm}

We integrate with vLLM 0.17.1 (v1 architecture) using its plugin system.  The
adapter monkey-patches \texttt{Scheduler.\_\_init\_\_} to inject a scheduler
hook at three points:

\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
\item \textbf{Pre-schedule: victim reorder + proactive preemption.}
  The hook sorts the running request list by a completion-aware \emph{keep
  score} that accounts for output progress, estimated remaining tokens,
  recompute cost, and an anti-starvation multiplier.  This ensures vLLM's
  native \texttt{running.pop()} preempts the lowest-value request.
  When KV utilization exceeds 88\%, the waiting queue is non-empty, and at
  least three requests are running, the hook proactively preempts the
  lowest-scored request with a 3-second cooldown.

\item \textbf{Post-decode tracking.}  After each step, newly sampled tokens
  are appended to per-request buffers for scoring.

\item \textbf{Lifecycle cleanup.}  On request completion, bids and state are
  cleared.
\end{enumerate}

\noindent
\textbf{Execution semantics (recompute fallback).}
When the solver selects a victim, the adapter calls vLLM's
\texttt{\_preempt\_request()}, releasing all KV blocks
and moving the request to the waiting queue for full
recomputation.  \bidkv does not modify vLLM's KV cache
coordinator or block pool---it only controls \emph{which}
request is preempted.  Under this model, all strategies
produce identical output quality; the differentiation lies
in scheduling efficiency.

\subsection{SGLang Integration}\label{sec:impl:sglang}

\section{Evaluation}\label{sec:eval}

We evaluate \bidkv along four dimensions: (1)~overall serving performance
(Section~\ref{sec:eval:main}), (2)~sensitivity to arrival rate
(Section~\ref{sec:eval:rate}), (3)~preemption event analysis
(Section~\ref{sec:eval:mechanism}), and (4)~tail latency breakdown
(Section~\ref{sec:eval:ttft}).

\subsection{Experimental Setup}\label{sec:eval:setup}

\paragraph{Hardware and model.}
All experiments run on a single NVIDIA RTX A6000 GPU (48\,GiB) with CUDA~12.5.
We serve Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct~\cite{Dubey2024} in bf16 precision
($\sim$16\,GiB model weight), leaving $\sim$32\,GiB for KV cache.

\paragraph{Inference engine.}
vLLM 0.17.1 (v1 architecture) with PagedAttention~\cite{Kwon2023} serves as the
primary quantitative platform.

\paragraph{Workload and traces.}
We use a \emph{long-context} workload: 500~requests with prompt length
$\geq 1024$~tokens sampled from the ShareGPT Vicuna unfiltered
dataset~\cite{ShareGPT2023}, whose length distribution is representative of
real-world LLM conversations~\cite{Zheng2024lmsys}.  Requests arrive according
to a Poisson process (open-loop) at three rates: $\{0.35, 0.5, 0.7\}$~req/s.
All traces are generated with seed~42 and verified via SHA-256 checksums; the
same traces are shared across all strategies.

\paragraph{SLO definition.}
A request meets its SLO if TTFT~$< 2000$\,ms and TPOT~$< 100$\,ms.

\paragraph{Baselines.}
Seven strategies form a complete attribution chain:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
\item \textbf{Preempt-Evict} (vLLM default): LIFO request-level preemption.
\item \textbf{Static-Random}: randomly selects preemption victims.
\item \textbf{H2O-Style}~\cite{Zhang2023h2o}: attention-based heavy-hitter
  scoring for victim selection.
\item \textbf{Uniform}: equal-weight victim scoring.
\item \textbf{Global-NoBid}: system-inferred scheduling without user bids.
\item \textbf{Slack-Aware}: SLO-deadline-aware victim selection.
\item \textbf{\bidkv}: full system (H2O scoring $\to$ bids $\to$ greedy solver).
\end{enumerate}

The chain enables precise ablation:
\emph{Preempt-Evict $\to$ H2O-Style $\to$ Global-NoBid $\to$ \bidkv}
isolates the value of token-level scoring, system-level coordination, and
explicit bids.  Each configuration is repeated three times with mean reported.

(3)~H2O-Style $\to$ BidKV (+8.6~pp SLO): the largest gap, showing that
    quality-aware cross-request coordination is critical---local token scoring
    without global coordination actively hurts performance.

\paragraph{Three operating regimes.}

\emph{Low load (rate=0.35):}
KV pressure is minimal; preemption is rarely triggered ($\leq$29 events across
all strategies).  All strategies achieve 91--92\% SLO attainment, confirming
that scheduling sophistication adds no value when pressure is absent.

\emph{Medium load (rate=0.5):}
KV pressure is moderate (85--95 proactive preemptions for active strategies).
\bidkv's quality-aware victim selection achieves 76.8\% SLO vs.\ H2O-Style's
68.2\%---the primary differentiating regime.

\emph{High load (rate=0.7):}
All strategies saturate and SLO drops below 10\%.  The key distinction is
between strategies that cause cascading recompute storms and those that do not.
\textbf{Conservative group} (Preempt-Evict: 8.8\%, Global-NoBid: 8.0\%,
BidKV: 8.2\%) maintains TTFT~P95 at 39--42\,s.
\textbf{Aggressive group} (H2O-Style: 3.6\%, Static-Random: 3.8\%, Uniform:
3.8\%, Slack-Aware: 3.8\%) suffers TTFT~P95 of 48--62\,s.

\paragraph{Why aggressive heuristics fail under high load.}
H2O-Style triggers 188 preemptions at rate=0.7 and frees the most tokens per
preemption (Figure~\ref{fig:preempt_analysis}), yet achieves the \emph{worst}
TTFT~P95 (61.8\,s).  Its token-level scoring identifies and preempts requests
with the highest attention-weight concentrations---but these are often the
requests closest to completion.  Preempting near-complete requests maximizes
tokens freed per event but triggers cascading recompute: the preempted
request must restart from scratch, consuming KV capacity anew and pressuring
the system to preempt again.  \bidkv avoids this trap by incorporating
recompute cost into its utility calculation, selecting victims whose
preemption causes the least cascading disruption.

\section{Discussion and Limitations}\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Surrogate Scheduling Cost}\label{sec:discuss:surrogate}

The quality delta~$\qualdelta$ in each bid is a \emph{surrogate parameter}
derived from token-level importance scores, not a direct measurement of
downstream output quality.  Under the recompute-fallback execution model,
preempted requests are fully recomputed by vLLM's native mechanism---the final
output is identical regardless of which strategy selected the victim.

$\qualdelta$ therefore controls \emph{scheduling aggressiveness}: a low
$\dbudget$ makes the solver conservative (fewer preemptions), while a high
$\dbudget$ allows more aggressive scheduling.  The solver does not trade output
quality for throughput; it trades preemption frequency and victim selection
quality for scheduling efficiency.

Calibrating $\qualdelta$ against task-level metrics (\eg ROUGE, exact match)
becomes relevant in a \emph{token-level truncation} execution model (Mode~B),
where preempted tokens are permanently discarded rather than recomputed.  We
leave this as future work.

\subsection{Limitations}

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,nosep]
\item \textbf{Single GPU.}  All experiments use a single A6000.  Multi-GPU
  deployments may introduce coordination overhead for bid collection.
\item \textbf{Single model scale (8B).}  Larger models (70B+) with different
  KV-to-weight ratios need further validation.
\item \textbf{Moderate differentiation at medium load.}  Against most baselines,
  \bidkv's SLO advantage at rate=0.5 is 1--2~pp.  The large gain
  ($+$8.6~pp) is primarily over H2O-Style; \bidkv's value proposition
  strengthens as scheduling sophistication increases.
\item \textbf{High-load saturation.}  At rate=0.7, all strategies drop below
  10\% SLO.  \bidkv mitigates but cannot prevent saturation; capacity
  scaling (\eg multi-GPU, request routing) is needed.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Future Work: Token-Level Truncation (Mode~B)}

The current recompute-fallback model (Mode~A) preserves output quality but
incurs full recomputation cost.  A token-level truncation model (Mode~B) would
permanently discard selected tokens rather than recomputing, creating a true
quality--throughput tradeoff where $\qualdelta$ directly measures output
degradation.  This requires kernel-level integration for partial KV block
release and task-level quality evaluation, which we leave as future work.

\subsection{Broader Impact}

The bid interface naturally supports \emph{SLO-differentiated serving}:
high-priority requests can express higher quality sensitivity, receiving stronger
protection against preemption.  Deployers should consider per-request preemption
caps or fairness-aware solver extensions~\cite{Ghodsi2011} to prevent systematic
starvation of low-priority requests.

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}

We presented \bidkv, a quality-aware preemption scheduling primitive for
online LLM serving.  By introducing explicit quality preference signals (bids)
into the preemption decision, \bidkv enables cross-request coordination that
selects victims based on the utility ratio
$\utilfn = \tokfree / (\qualdelta + \stabeps)$ rather than arbitrary LIFO
ordering or fixed heuristics.

Through a seven-baseline attribution chain on vLLM with
Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct, we demonstrated that \bidkv achieves 76.8\% SLO
attainment---13\% higher than H2O-Style---and reduces P95~TTFT by 35\% under
high load.  The results reveal that aggressive token-level heuristics can
actively harm system performance when used for preemption scheduling without
global coordination, while quality-aware victim selection avoids cascading
recompute storms.

\bidkv's framework-portable design integrates with both vLLM and SGLang without
source-code modifications, requiring zero external dependencies.  Future work
includes token-level truncation for direct quality--throughput tradeoff,
fairness-aware solver extensions, and multi-GPU coordination.
